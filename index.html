Procrastination: what is it, and how to overcome this issue


JiWon Lee | Daily Trojan

Procrastination is more scientifically than you think

Procrastination has been a huge issue since the beginning of humanity; lamentably, we do not have evidence that humans started procrastinating since we became conciseness about our existence. However, we do have a piece of information that leads us to know that procrastination was an issue even before the existence of the technology that makes us in the present day super distracted. Approximately in 700 B.C., an ancient Greek poet, Hesiod, proclaimed in a poem called "Work and Days." He expressed, "Do not put your work off till tomorrow and the day after." The sentence is clear evidence that during the 8th century, procrastination was already an issue for humans, but is this evidence enough to claim that procrastination existed long before the new technologies appeared? During the 8th century, poetry was one of the fundamental ways of expressing ideas to the public. Also, poetry is based on human dilemmas on issues such as love, death, life, and others. Hence, poetry was a respectful skill that not everyone could do, only notable people during that time, which means that procrastination existed long ago.

Nevertheless, procrastination is already in our genes; our ancestors used to fight, flee, feed or mate when they felt the urge to do it, as lions or other animals do. They eat, hunt, or play among them when they felt doing it; there is no plan, no future, and they only live the present and do what their bodies want at that moment. However, imagination plays a significant role in this scenario; it lets humans develop an enormous sense of the future, see reality, imagine how it could be different, and translate those imaginations into a new reality. We can prepare for the hypothetical future, and we even have the conciseness that if we continue doing the same action every day, something will happen, either bad or good. We have a morality that affects our feelings, making us feel unproductive and sometimes useless, which is an indicator when we do something that we know is not worth it. Unlike animals, when they rest all day, they do not feel the same feeling as us. Hence, despite our mental time travel skill to predict the future based on our past experiences and our level of self-awareness, self-reflection, and morality that regulates our feelings as an indicator of productivity, comes the question, why do we still procrastinate?



What is procrastination?

New Africa/Shutterstock

"Procrastination is the act of delaying or putting off tasks until the last minute, or past their deadline" (Cherry). Similarly, the dictionary defines procrastination as "the action of delaying or postponing something." We can agree that procrastination's definition is easy to understand; it is just unnecessarily postponing things and actions for later because we do not "feel" like doing them. Later in the essay, I will explain why this happens, but first, let us understand why procrastination is a big deal and how it affects us.


Why procrastination is a big deal?

KEDI Healthcare

Procrastination is dangerous and has many adverse effects such as decrement in academic performance, worse financial status, a rise of issues in relationships with people, reduced well-being, and worse mental and physical health, plus the feeling of being useless. Procrastination is addictive because the more someone procrastinates, the more they will develop the habit of being lazy; it is like procrastination generates more procrastination. Hence, procrastination is a vicious cycle; if an individual does not do anything today, that individual will be stuck the next day. This lack of action leads to more procrastination, which makes the individual feel useless, plus that feeling and thinking of "I have not done anything today yet, so why am I going to start now? So, just let's take a day off." And so on, every day will become a day off.


Causes of why we procrastinate

solvingprocrastination.com

Why does this happens? Why do we keep procrastinating? If we know, it is terrible for us, and even sometimes, we are conscious that we are not doing what we should. Thankfully, there is a science behind it. Some studies in an article by Priya Babu concluded that students with high self-esteem procrastinate less, whereas those who procrastinate more have comparatively lower self-esteem. Also, other studies revealed that the human brain is wired to be lazy. According to Hindustan Times, a new study showed that our brains may simply be wired to prefer lying on the couch and that human brains work harder to pick physical activity over relaxation. Humans have been connected to laziness since the beginning of time; that is why we are so greedy sometimes because we only want a simple and easy life by resting and relaxing while doing nothing. Hence, procrastination is an action our brain uses to stop thinking and enjoy the "free" time.

Furthermore, procrastination is in our genes of survival as well because human brains are programmed to conserve energy, the process of conserving energy has been essential for our survival long time ago with this energy conserved, we were more efficient in searching for food, making shelter, mate, and most importantly, avoiding predators. According to Ewan McNay, the brain burns more energy during an intense cognitive task than you would vegging out watching TV or whatever. These two statements are essential to put together; our brain loves not to do anything that takes much energy and effort because of our survival genes of storing energy, and this is because solid cognitive tasks make our brain lose energy.


Pleasure hormone (Dopamine)

Samantha Lee/Business Insider

Dopamine hormone is known as the "pleasure hormone" because it gives us a sense of pleasure and the motivation to do something. Practically, Dopamine is a reward from our brain when we do something we like. "Dopamine plays a role in how we feel pleasure. It's a big part of our unique human ability to think and plan. It helps us strive, focus, and find things interesting." (Cristol). Thus, Dopamine is our primary reward dose from our brain when we do something our brain likes. One of these things is just resting and doing nothing, like sitting on the couch and watching some TV series with a piece of chocolate on our side and a good Coca-Cola next to it; our brain generates a high level of Dopamine, and not only because our brain likes it, also because that bar of chocolate and Coca-Cola generates Dopamine as well. So, we are getting a big reward of Dopamine (that feeling of pleasure) at that moment, as well as the feeling that we have nothing to worry about and just "enjoy" our "free" time, while in our background life, we have more than hundred things to do and finish it. However, we say, "why would I worry about it? If I still have one week to complete it," the problem is that we think that we have one week, but instead, we are only having one day because, at that moment, that we left ourselves on that couch assuming that we have time, that action and choice will still occur until the deadline. To be clear, I am not saying that we have to complete everything weeks before the deadline; I am just saying that we must realize that these actions and thought processes happen, that this kind of behavior our brain loves, and the brain will do anything and excuse with many different thoughts to avoid doing what we must do.


Procrastination as a habit

thehabitstation.com

Dopamine reward is not the only way of getting to procrastinate more and more; Dopamine is just the starting point of something worse. What is worse than Dopamine is habits, which is what we practice regularly and is hard to give up. Here is when it gets complicated because we can have our doses of Dopamine by avoiding tasks that our brain finds hard to do, but once is okay, as well as twice, but when we start to do it regularly, we also start to create a habit, and what is even worse is that we do not even realize that we are creating the habit. In the book of Charles Duhigg, "The Power of Habit," he explains that creating a habit consist of three parts: A cue, a routine, and a reward. To translate this into our example of creating a procrastination habit, we have a: New task with a deadline due one week from now (cue), "I have time" thought without even checking how hard it would be the task (routine), and go to distract ourselves by doing an effortless thing (reward).

Furthermore, how long do we have to do it to form this new habit of procrastinating? According to a 2009 European Journal of Social Psychology study, "The average modelled time to plateau in this sample was 66 days, but the range was from 18 to 254 days." (Lally 1007). Hence, if we do this for 66 days straight (average), congratulations, we have created a new habit, the habit of procrastination. Once this habit is created, it is super hard to stop, so it is essential to realize that we are procrastinators and train our brain to figure out when the cue is coming to avoid the routine and the reward that leads to creating the habit of procrastination.


We can stop it!

Eli Savage | Staff Illustrator

Procrastination is usual; we cannot blame or feel guilty about ourselves when we sometimes procrastinate because it is normal behavior, and sometimes it is even healthy to enjoy doing effortless things so we can clear our minds, and each person has a way of doing this. The issue here is what kind of effortless things we do to clear our minds. Some people play instruments, others meditate, and some go to workouts, among many other activities. However, there are as well plenty of people who, instead of clearing their minds and doing nothing for a moment by doing something that at the same time is "healthy" or "fruitfulness" for their lives, grab their phone and open Instagram or TikTok and they spend 2 to 4 hours there and sometimes even more. So, the issues are time spent when procrastinating, what we do to procrastinate, and most importantly, how regularly we are. If we procrastinate just one day, but by doing something healthy for us, it is okay, but it is essential to start doing the task as soon as possible during that day or the next day. However, what if we are already addictive procrastinators but want to stop being postponers. How do we overcome procrastination?


Overcoming procrastination

Step 1

We must forgive ourselves because we procrastinated in the past. Some studies show that "Results revealed that among students who reported high levels of self-forgiveness for procrastinating on studying for the first examination, procrastination on preparing for the subsequent examination was reduced." (Wohl). Thus, self-forgiveness can help us to feel more positive about ourselves and reduce future procrastination. In addition, another tactic is to practice self-compassion, which is treating ourselves with kindness when trying to understand our mistakes and failures. To support this idea of self-compassion, a study by Dr. Sirois found that lower levels of self-compassion may explain some of the stress experienced by procrastinators and interventions that promote self-compassion could therefore be beneficial for these individuals. These tactics proven by studies are all connected to our self-esteem because to first feel worth, we need to forgive ourselves from the past to progress and try to get better, and self-esteem is crucial in order to avoid procrastination. When we procrastinate, our brain takes that task as a threat to our self-esteem because if we overthink the task, we may end up with the conclusion that it is a difficult task, and it will generate the fear of not being able to accomplish it because of the "I am not smart" thought, without even read what the task about is. Ironically, the best way to stop those fears is action.


Step 2

We need to understand what procrastination is and why it is caused; in that way, unconsciously, we will be able to identify and recognize when we are about to procrastinate; we will be able to identify that cue that makes us want to start a routine, once we identify the cue, we will be able to stop at that moment and avoid doing the routine.


Step 3

Once the cue is identified and we know that we want to procrastinate, we need to combat a little against our brain and try to hack our brain in order to make our brain find worthy of doing the task right now; one good way of doing it is to tell ourselves "I will just do it for 5 minutes, then I am free to stop." If the task is not enjoyable for that person, the chances of stopping after 5 minutes are high. However, if that person does that again after an hour or even just the next day, there will be a moment where, in most cases, after the timer stops, most of the people, knowing that they could stop if they wanted to, they will give them a little extra time and incentive to continue for at least a little more minutes. On the other hand, another hack people can use to avoid procrastination is just starting it; what do I mean by this? I am talking about a rule that, according to Mel Robbins in her book "The 5 Seconds Rule," when an opportunity arises, or you must do something, do not think about it; just count 5-4-3-2-1 and decide. After five seconds, your brain will start to betray you, and it will kill your motivation to do what you must do. If a person wants to run every morning, when waking up, instead of thinking if it will be a good idea, just count 5-4-3-2-1 and go. Otherwise, our brain will have the chance to overthink it, and overthinking is never good. However, do not confuse it with thinking logically; this rule is good when we need to take any action we know we must do; otherwise, we will feel bad if we do not do it. However, these actions must be thought out clearly before making this choice. As an example, I know that eating sugar is not suitable for me. So first, I will think logically about and even research why overeating sugar is terrible for the body (that is the logical thinking I made), then I confirm that choice and start avoiding sugar. Then I go out with friends to have dinner, and I get the chance to eat sugar in a dessert, so I feel tempted by it because I have already had that habit of eating a dessert every time I go to have dinner, and that is the moment of doing the 5 seconds rule. At first, there was logical thinking to decide the choice that a person will make from now on, then to make the action the 5 minutes rule will be helpful. Also, one of the best ways to avoid procrastination is to avoid distractions, active focus mode, or turn off smartphones and finish what you must do.


Step 4

Is essential always to promise our brain that we will give it a reward after doing what we were planning to do. The thing to notice here is that it has to be something your brain wants. It can be anything, while, of course, it is not something that will destroy you, like drugs or any other unhealthy thing. What I found helpful during this practice is that I used to tell my brain that I was going to play PlayStation after finishing my homework until I got bored of playing. So, the reward at first worked as a trick to hack the brain to make it do the task, and then after finishing the task, change the reward at the last moment. What was more interesting is that this reward worked in the same way as the 5 minutes rule because after I finished my homework, I was feeling already productive, practical, and super motivated to continue being productive, so instead of playing video games, I ended up doing online courses, or working out at the gym, or hanging out with my friends.


Step 5

We must self-sabotage our thoughts. The way of doing this is to change the way we speak to ourselves; instead of saying "I have to do this task," we should change to "I choose to do this task," this works because we humans are likely not to obey easily, and mostly when we are obligated to do something that we do not want to. We like to blame others or delegate work that we should do to others because the task was not our choice. However, if we trick our brain by letting it know that we choose to do something, the brain will focus on doing it because we let the brain feel the freedom of choice and not the feeling of being obligated to do it. Thus, when we want to stop procrastinating, we could trick the brain by saying, "I want to do this task" or "I choose to do this task," and honestly believe it. Otherwise, it will not work; we need motivation, and what better motivation than genuinely wanting to do something.


Conclusion

Procrastination is something that affects all of us; there are multiple ways of procrastinating, and the main idea is to choose in which way we want to procrastinate. Plus, we can avoid procrastination by applying different thinking processes or rule techniques like "The 5 Second Rule" or "The 5 Minutes Rule." It is also essential to notice that procrastination is a problem everyone has, and we should not blame others for not accomplishing a task by the due date. People need to understand procrastination, the science behind it, and how to avoid this problem because it is not super easy to stop, but at the same time, it is not super complicated to stop.
